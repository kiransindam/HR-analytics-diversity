Persistent senior-level male-dominated hiring creates a structural ceiling. Over 5 years, this compounds into an organisation where female talent is abundant at the base but invisible at the top — damaging retention and employer brand.

Business Action: Implement targeted senior-level talent sourcing from women's leadership networks (e.g., Women in Leadership India, Women on Boards). Set measurable hiring targets: aim for 25% female Executive hires within 2 years. Audit job descriptions at senior levels for gendered language using tools like Textio. Key Data Findings

What it Means

A 9.4% turnover rate is moderate but the key finding is that leavers are not low-performers — they're often mid-to-high performers leaving voluntarily. This is the most costly type of attrition.

Risk If Ignored

Voluntary mid-performer attrition at 9.4% annually costs the organisation approximately 1.5–2x each leaver's annual salary in replacement costs. Over 3 years, that's potentially €3–5M in avoidable cost.

Business Action: Implement structured exit interviews with data capture by gender, level, and reason. Introduce stay interviews for mid-to-senior female employees at 18-month and 3-year tenure marks. Segment turnover by gender to identify whether women leave at higher rates than men at any specific level. Consider retention bonuses or accelerated career path signals for high-performing employees at turnover-risk levels.

This is the most important KPI in the entire dashboard. Women perform equally to men across all rating bands and the averages are statistically tied. Any disparity in hiring, promotion, or pay equity cannot be justified by performance differences.

Lead every D&I conversation with this KPI. It's your most powerful evidence that the organisation has a fairness problem, not a talent problem. Equal performance + unequal advancement = structural inequity.

Business Action: Use KPI 4 as the foundation of your business case for D&I investment. Present the comparison: "Women rate 2.42, Men rate 2.41 — yet women hold only 12.5% of executive roles." Conduct a formal pay equity audit using performance ratings as one of the control variables. Share performance parity data with managers during promotion calibration to counter unconscious bias.

What the Charts Show

Four donut charts side by side showing: (1) Executive team gender split at start of FY20 — 87.5% Male, 12.5% Female. (2) Executive hires in FY20 — 100% Male. (3) Executive split at end of FY21 — 84.21% Male, 15.79% Female. (4) Promotions to Executive in FY21 — 100% Male.

Key Data Findings

Zero female Executive hires in FY20. Zero female promotions to Executive in FY21. The percentage of female executives only improved marginally from 12.5% to 15.79% — and this slight improvement was not from promotion or external hiring in the tracked year.

What it Means

The executive pipeline for women is functionally closed. Two full fiscal years with zero female executive appointments (either hired or promoted) means the 12.5% female executive representation will erode further as current female executives move on.

Risk If Ignored

With current trajectory, female executive representation will reach 0% within 5–8 years. This creates legal, reputational, and strategic risk — particularly as ESG reporting requirements increasingly include gender diversity at board and executive level.

Business Action: Declare a measurable executive gender target: e.g., 30% female executives by 2027 (Catalyst benchmark). Create a named female executive succession pipeline — identify 3–5 high-potential senior women today. Make all executive searches 50/50 long-listed by gender as a minimum requirement. Tie executive bonus structures to D&I KPI achievement. Report this KPI externally in the Annual Report.

A majority-young workforce with limited 50+ representation creates succession and knowledge transfer risks. As the 20–29 cohort advances, without structural D&I improvements, the gender gaps at senior level will simply replicate themselves.

Business Action: Design mentorship and career development programmes specifically for the 20–29 cohort. Create reverse mentoring pairings (junior employees mentoring executives on new thinking). Build a knowledge management system before the small 50+ cohort retires. Monitor age + gender intersectionality — are young women advancing at the same rate as young men?

05 · Presentation Guide

How to Present This Project — A to Z

A complete playbook for delivering this D&I dashboard presentation with authority — from preparation through follow-up.

Step 1 · 48 Hours Before
Preparation & Mental Framing

Memorise the 6 core numbers: 500 employees, 41% female hires, 9.4% turnover, 2.42 vs 2.41 performance ratings, 29.9% female promotions, 12.5% female executives.

Write down the one-sentence "so what" for each KPI. Practice saying it out loud without looking at the dashboard.

Prepare your "headline statement" — the single sentence that frames the whole presentation. Suggested: "Our data proves we have a talent fairness problem, not a talent shortage — women perform identically to men but advance at half the rate."

Prepare the Power BI file on the presentation device. Test all 4 slicers (Age Group, Region, Job Level, Department). Know which filter combinations produce the most striking insights for your audience.

Step 2 · Opening (2–3 min)
Hook the Room Immediately

Do NOT open with "I'd like to present our HR analytics dashboard." Open with the data:

"Our female employees have a higher average performance rating than our male employees. And yet, men receive 70% of all promotions. That gap is what this dashboard is here to fix."

This creates immediate engagement — everyone in the room will have an opinion. You own the conversation from that moment.

Then state the agenda: 3 dashboard pages, 6 KPIs, 6 recommendations, 20 minutes total. Invite questions at the end rather than interrupting the data story.

Step 3 · Dashboard Walk-Through (12–15 min)
Navigate in This Exact Order for Maximum Impact

1. Start with Page 3: KPI 4 (Performance) — Lead with equal performance. This is your evidence base. Establish the fact before presenting the inequality.

2. Move to Page 1: Overview — Show the 500 headcount, 59:41 ratio, and the 70:30 promotion split. Let the gap land visually.

3. Go to Page 3: KPI 5 (Executive Balance) — The four donuts showing zero female executive appointments are the most striking visual in the whole report. Present this as the consequence of the promotion gap.

4. Back to Page 2: KPI 1 (Hiring) — Show that the problem starts at hiring. 13% female Executive hires — use the slicer to filter to just Executive level for impact.

5. Page 2: KPI 2 (Promotions) — Reinforce: even internal promotion is inequitable. Show the time-in-grade data — women wait longer.

6. Close with KPI 3 (Turnover) — Link attrition to the equity story. Mid-performers leaving = likely D&I-related attrition.

For each KPI use: "What you see → what it means → what we need to do." Never show data without the "what we need to do" conclusion.

Step 4 · Recommendations (4–5 min)
Present Actions, Not Problems

Summarise the 6 recommendations in priority order (Critical → High → Medium). For each, state: the action, who owns it, a measurable target, and the timeline.

Frame the investment: "If we can raise female promotions to 45%, we reduce D&I-related attrition risk and avoid €1.5–3M in annual replacement costs. This is not a cost — it's a return on investment."

Step 5 · Interactive Demo (optional, 3 min)
Use the Slicers Strategically

Demonstrate the Power BI interactivity live. Useful combinations to show:

Filter to Executive only: Shows how stark the imbalance is at the very top.

Filter to Switzerland only: Shows whether the home market has the same or worse patterns than other regions.

Filter to 20–29 age group: Shows the future workforce and where investment will have the most long-term impact.

Step 6 · Q&A (5–10 min)
Handle Pushback With Data, Not Emotion

D&I dashboards frequently generate defensive responses. Prepare for:

"We hire the best person regardless of gender." — Response: "Agreed — and the performance data shows women are equally 'best'. The hiring and promotion patterns suggest we're not always reaching them."

"This is an industry-wide problem." — Response: "That's true and also not a solution. Our competitors who fix this first will win the talent war."

"The sample size is small at executive level." — Response: "Correct — which is exactly why we need to widen it."

Step 7 · Within 24 Hours
Post-Presentation Follow-Up

Send the follow-up email (template in Section 07) with this documentation package and the .pbix file attached.

Include agreed actions, owners, and next review date in writing. Schedule a 6-week progress check on the Critical recommendations.

06 · Stakeholder Communication

Tailoring Your Message by Audience

Your manager and your client have fundamentally different motivations. Here is exactly how to communicate with each to maximise buy-in and action.

👔
Your Manager
Internal · Process quality, credibility, team performance

What they care about: Was the analysis methodology sound? Is the Power BI model well-structured? Does this reflect positively on the team? What are next steps?

What to lead with: Your approach — data model design, KPI selection rationale, interactivity features. Show you've thought about the "so what" not just the "what."

Tone: Collaborative and ownership-driven. Say "the analysis shows" not "I think." Proactively flag data limitations or gaps.

Key message: "The dashboard is complete, the 6 KPIs are all powered by clean DAX measures, and the interactivity allows the client to explore by age, region, level, and department in real-time. I've identified 6 recommendations ready to present."

Format: 15-minute review meeting or written summary. Don't over-present — let the dashboard and this documentation speak.

🏢
The Client (HR / C-Suite)
External · Business outcomes, compliance, talent strategy

What they care about: What does this mean for us? What do we do about it? Are we compliant? How do we compare to industry benchmarks? What will this cost to fix?

What to lead with: The business case — equal performance, unequal advancement, real cost of inaction. Always anchor to numbers and business outcomes.

Tone: Confident and direct. Avoid HR jargon. Say "men receive 2.3× more promotions than equally-performing women" not "there is a promotion equity gap."

Key message: "Your data shows a structural fairness problem that is costing you talent and money. The good news: it's fixable with 6 specific actions. The dashboard gives you the real-time visibility to track progress."

Format: 30-minute live Power BI presentation + this documentation as leave-behind. Allow 10 minutes of live exploration with the slicers — it builds ownership of the data.

⚖️
Legal / Compliance Team
Internal · Risk, regulation, reporting obligations

What they care about: Is the 0% female executive appointment data a legal risk? Are we meeting gender pay reporting requirements? What's our ESG disclosure exposure?

What to lead with: KPI 5 (Executive Balance) and the promotion gap. These are the two most legally sensitive findings. Frame in terms of documented risk.

Tone: Precise and evidence-based. Reference relevant regulation (EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, Gender Pay Gap legislation by country).

Key message: "This dashboard provides the audit trail and ongoing monitoring capability to demonstrate good faith progress on gender equity — which is increasingly material to regulatory compliance and ESG reporting."

👥
Line Managers & HR Business Partners
Operational · Day-to-day people decisions

What they care about: What does this mean for my team? Am I expected to change how I make decisions? Will this be used to evaluate my performance?

What to lead with: The positive framing — this data is about improving fairness and keeping your best people. Then show KPI 4 (equal performance) to establish the foundation.

Tone: Supportive and practical. Focus on tools and processes they can use: calibration guidelines, interview frameworks, promotion templates.

Key message: "This dashboard helps you make better decisions with data. It's not about blame — it's about building the most successful team possible from the full range of available talent."

08 · FAQ

Anticipated Questions & Prepared Answers

Prepare for these before every presentation. D&I dashboards generate strong reactions — having data-backed answers ready makes the difference between a good presentation and a great one.

The performance ratings are nearly identical — does that really prove bias?
Answer: Near-identical performance ratings (2.42 vs 2.41) combined with a 70:30 promotion split is one of the strongest statistical signals of promotion bias available. If men and women perform equally, any systematic difference in advancement rates is by definition not explained by merit. The combination of equal performance + unequal promotion + zero female executive appointments creates a pattern, not a coincidence. Statistical significance would require deeper regression analysis controlling for additional variables, which we can scope as a phase 2 if needed.
Why is the female executive percentage shown as 8700% promoted in FY20?
Answer: This figure is almost certainly a DAX calculation artefact — likely a percentage-of-percentage measure that hasn't been formatted correctly for the context, or a measure that calculates promotion rate as a percentage of a very small base (e.g., 1 promotion from a base of very few females in that category). In the live presentation, acknowledge this and note it as a data model refinement to address. The underlying promotion data in KPI 2 is the reliable source for promotion analysis.
Is 9.4% turnover actually a problem — isn't some turnover healthy?
Answer: The benchmark for healthy turnover varies by industry — typically 10–15% is considered normal, making 9.4% within range. However, the critical question is not the rate but who is leaving and why. The KPI 3 data shows mid-to-high performers are leaving (their ratings at departure are not significantly below non-leavers). Voluntary mid-performer attrition — regardless of rate — is the most costly kind, typically running 1.5–2× annual salary per leaver to replace. At 47 leavers, that's a potential €2–5M annual cost depending on salary levels. The priority is segmenting turnover by gender to identify if D&I factors are a driver.
The data is from FY2020–2021 — isn't it too old to act on?
Answer: Two-year data is actually valuable precisely because it shows consistent trends rather than one-off variation. The fact that both FY20 and FY21 show identical 59:41 promotion splits (and zero female executive appointments in both years) makes the pattern statistically stronger, not weaker. The dashboard is designed to be refreshed with current data — updating the underlying Excel data source will automatically update all visuals. Recommend quarterly refreshes going forward.
We operate in Switzerland — isn't this normal for the industry/region?
Answer: Switzerland has made significant progress on gender diversity regulation in recent years. The Gender Equality Act (2021) requires companies with 100+ employees to conduct pay equity analyses. The Swiss Code of Best Practice recommends 30% gender diversity at board level. More practically: the Catalyst Global Scorecard shows organisations with higher female leadership outperform their sector by 15–20% on EBITDA. "Industry normal" is becoming "industry lagging" very quickly — and talent competition is not constrained by regional norms.
What would it actually cost to fix this and what's the ROI?
Answer: The interventions required are primarily process changes (calibration frameworks, panel diversity, sponsorship programmes) — not large budget items. External executive search with gender requirements adds 10–15% to search fees but pays back immediately in the quality of hire. The ROI calculation is straightforward: if improving D&I reduces voluntary mid-performer attrition by 20% (8 fewer leavers annually), at an estimated replacement cost of €80–120K per leaver, that's €640K–€960K saved per year. The investment in process changes is far less than this. Additionally, McKinsey's Diversity Wins report shows top-quartile gender-diverse companies are 25% more likely to outperform peers on profitability.
Can the dashboard track progress over time?
Answer: Yes — this is one of the most powerful features of the Power BI approach. As new HR data is added to the source file (Excel), all visuals refresh automatically. We can add a date slicer to enable year-over-year comparison. We recommend setting up quarterly data refreshes and creating a "trend" page showing key KPIs over rolling 12 months. This transforms the dashboard from a point-in-time snapshot to a live management tool.
